Frequently Asked Questions

Science-based answers to common fitness questions

In the fitness world, there are countless debates, myths, and conflicting information. At Ascend Coaching, we base our recommendations on scientific evidence, peer-reviewed research, and proven methodologies. This FAQ addresses common questions and debates within science-based fitness communities, helping you navigate the noise and focus on what actually works.

Training & Programming

What's the best training split: PPL, Upper/Lower, or Full Body?

The answer: It depends on your experience level, schedule, and goals.

The science shows that training frequency and volume matter more than the specific split. Here's what research tells us:

  • Beginners: Full body or upper/lower splits work best, allowing 2-3x per week frequency per muscle group
  • Intermediate: Upper/lower or PPL splits (3-6x per week) allow for more volume and specialization
  • Advanced: Any split can work if volume, intensity, and recovery are properly managed

The key is hitting each muscle group 2-3x per week with adequate volume (10-20 sets per muscle group per week) and progressive overload. The split is just a tool to organize your training.

How many sets per muscle group per week is optimal?

Research from Dr. Mike Israetel and others suggests:

  • Beginners: 10-12 sets per muscle group per week
  • Intermediate: 12-18 sets per muscle group per week
  • Advanced: 18-22+ sets per muscle group per week (with proper recovery)

These are general guidelines. Individual response varies based on genetics, recovery capacity, training age, and other factors. The key is progressive overload - gradually increasing volume, intensity, or frequency over time.

Is progressive overload the only way to build muscle?

Progressive overload is the primary driver, but not the only factor.

Muscle growth requires:

  • Mechanical tension: Progressive overload (more weight, reps, or sets over time)
  • Metabolic stress: The "pump" and fatigue from training
  • Muscle damage: Controlled damage that triggers repair and growth

While progressive overload is essential, you can achieve it through various methods: increasing weight, reps, sets, training frequency, or reducing rest times. The method matters less than the consistent progression.

Do I need to train to failure for maximum gains?

The science: Training close to failure (1-3 reps in reserve) is effective, but training to absolute failure isn't necessary for most sets.

Research shows:

  • Training to 1-3 RIR (reps in reserve) provides similar hypertrophy to training to failure
  • Training to failure increases fatigue and may limit volume in subsequent sets
  • Occasional failure training can be useful, but constant failure training may hinder recovery

Most science-based coaches recommend leaving 1-3 reps in the tank on most sets, with occasional failure sets for intensity techniques.

Nutrition & Diet

How much protein do I really need for muscle growth?

The consensus: 0.7-1.0g per pound of bodyweight (1.6-2.2g per kg) is optimal for most people.

Research consistently shows:

  • Higher protein intake (up to 1g/lb) supports muscle growth and recovery
  • Protein timing matters less than total daily intake
  • Spreading protein across 3-4 meals (20-40g per meal) may optimize muscle protein synthesis
  • Going above 1g/lb provides minimal additional benefits for most people

For cutting phases, higher protein (up to 1.2g/lb) helps preserve muscle mass. For bulking, 0.7-0.9g/lb is typically sufficient.

Do I need to eat every 2-3 hours to maintain muscle?

No. Meal timing frequency has minimal impact on muscle growth for most people.

The science shows:

  • Total daily protein intake matters far more than meal frequency
  • Muscle protein synthesis stays elevated for 3-5 hours after a protein meal
  • Eating 3-6 meals per day works fine - choose what fits your lifestyle
  • Intermittent fasting can work for muscle building if protein intake is adequate

Focus on hitting your daily protein and calorie targets. Meal timing is a tool for convenience and adherence, not a requirement for growth.

Are carbs necessary for muscle growth, or can I go low-carb?

Carbs aren't strictly necessary, but they're highly beneficial for performance and recovery.

Here's what the research shows:

  • Carbs replenish muscle glycogen, improving training performance
  • Better training performance = more volume = more growth potential
  • Carbs can spare protein from being used for energy
  • Low-carb diets can work, but may limit training intensity and volume

For most people, moderate to high carbs (2-4g per pound of bodyweight) support better training and recovery. Low-carb can work, but you may need to accept slightly lower training performance.

Supplements & Science

Which supplements actually work, and which are just marketing?

Evidence-based supplements with strong research support:

  • Creatine: Most researched supplement, proven to increase strength and muscle mass
  • Protein powder: Convenient way to hit protein targets (not magic, just convenient)
  • Caffeine: Proven performance enhancer
  • Beta-Alanine: Effective for high-rep, high-intensity training
  • Citrulline Malate: May improve performance and blood flow

Supplements with weak or mixed evidence:

  • BCAAs (if you eat enough protein, they're redundant)
  • Most "testosterone boosters" (minimal evidence, often overhyped)
  • Fat burners (caffeine works, but most ingredients are ineffective)

Remember: Supplements supplement your training and nutrition. They don't replace the fundamentals.

Do "testosterone boosters" actually work?

The honest answer: Most have minimal to no effect on testosterone levels in healthy individuals.

Here's what the science says:

  • Most "test boosters" show small, often insignificant increases in testosterone
  • These increases rarely translate to meaningful muscle or strength gains
  • Some ingredients (like D-Aspartic Acid, Tongkat Ali) may have small effects, but results are inconsistent
  • If you have clinically low testosterone, see a doctor - supplements won't fix that

Focus on the basics: adequate sleep, stress management, proper nutrition, and consistent training. These have far more impact on hormone health than most supplements.

Common Debates in Fitness Communities

High volume vs. low volume training - which is better?

Both can work, but optimal volume depends on individual factors.

The debate:

  • High volume advocates: More sets = more growth stimulus
  • Low volume advocates: Quality over quantity, less is more

The science: There's a dose-response relationship - more volume generally means more growth, up to a point. However:

  • Recovery capacity varies by individual
  • Training age matters (beginners need less volume)
  • There's a point of diminishing returns
  • Optimal volume is individual and changes over time

Start with moderate volume (12-15 sets per muscle group per week) and adjust based on recovery and progress. More isn't always better, but adequate volume is essential.

Is "bro-splits" (one muscle group per day) effective?

Bro-splits can work, but they're often suboptimal for most people.

The issue with traditional bro-splits:

  • Each muscle group is trained only once per week
  • Research shows 2-3x per week frequency is generally better for hypertrophy
  • You're leaving gains on the table with lower frequency

However: If a bro-split allows you to train with higher volume per session and you enjoy it, it can still work. The best program is one you'll stick to consistently.

For most people, upper/lower or PPL splits (hitting each muscle 2x per week) are more time-efficient and effective.

Should I bulk or cut first? What's the science-based approach?

It depends on your current body composition and goals.

General guidelines:

  • If you're overweight (20%+ body fat for men, 30%+ for women): Cut first to improve insulin sensitivity and health markers
  • If you're underweight or very lean: Bulk first to build muscle mass
  • If you're in the middle (12-18% body fat for men, 20-28% for women): Either can work, but a small surplus (lean bulk) is often preferred

The science: You can build muscle in a slight deficit if you're new to training or returning after a break. However, a small surplus (200-500 calories) generally supports better muscle growth for most people.

Most science-based coaches recommend shorter cycles (8-16 weeks) of bulking and cutting rather than year-long phases.

What's the deal with "muscle confusion" and changing programs frequently?

Muscle confusion is largely a myth. Progressive overload is what drives growth.

The concept: Constantly changing exercises confuses muscles, forcing adaptation.

The reality: Research shows:

  • Consistency and progressive overload matter more than exercise variety
  • Muscles adapt to progressive overload, not "confusion"
  • Too much variety can prevent progressive overload (hard to track progress)
  • Some exercise variation is good, but not constant change

Best practice: Stick with a program for 8-16 weeks, focus on progressive overload, then make strategic changes (not random ones) when progress stalls.

Science-Based Fitness Philosophy

Why do we focus on science-based approaches?

Because evidence beats anecdotes, and results matter more than opinions.

Science-based fitness means:

  • Basing recommendations on peer-reviewed research, not bro-science or marketing
  • Understanding that individual responses vary, but general principles apply
  • Being honest about what we know vs. what we don't know
  • Focusing on what works for most people, most of the time
  • Being open to new evidence and changing recommendations when better data emerges

This doesn't mean we ignore experience or practical knowledge - it means we combine the best of both: scientific evidence and real-world application.

How do I navigate conflicting information in fitness?

Focus on principles, not methods. Look for consensus, not outliers.

Key principles that have strong consensus:

  • Progressive overload is essential for growth
  • Adequate protein intake supports muscle building
  • Recovery (sleep, nutrition, stress management) is crucial
  • Consistency beats perfection
  • Individual response varies - what works for one person may not work for another

Red flags to watch for:

  • Claims that sound too good to be true
  • People selling products with extreme claims
  • Information that contradicts established scientific consensus
  • Advice that ignores individual differences

When in doubt, stick to the fundamentals: progressive overload, adequate nutrition, proper recovery, and consistency. These principles rarely change.
